## Follow the Leader (Part I)

No technical writer's skill set is complete without reading the words in the work of other technical writers and technical content developers. I didn't get to where I am today by being entirely siloed. It used to be that your only sources for learning and growing would be to attend STC (Society for Technical Communications) meetings, conferences, your own small network of writers (or team, or both), and maybe the library or your book collection. That isn't to say that those aren't valuable resources—I spent years learning that way—but now with the robust information ecosystem we have with social media sites like Linkedin and Substack, We can follow, watch, listen, and learn from technical writers across the globe!

I'd like to share a few of my favorites here. I don't intend the list to be limited to just these, I've intentionally named the post part one because I'd like to make this a series. There's a lot to learn and read from this list however, enough to keep you occupied for a while (even I have some catching up to do.). I also don't intend to keep these lists limited to technical writers. Kraft aside, There's a lot of stuff worth learning from other professionals, even outside of the tech industry.

<!-- more -->

So without further ado:

**Fabrizio Ferri Benedetti**

[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/fabrizioferri/) | [Website](https://passo.uno/)

I consider Fabrizio The "All-Star" of perpetually online technical writing gurus. He's a more developer-focused technical writer (and UX, and programmer)—there's a lot of focus in his articles on permutations of the "Docs As Code" methodology in technical documentation. He's created tools that he often [shares with his readers](https://passo.uno/build-tech-writing-tools-llms/) and fellow technical communicators, and advocated for ways we need to work with AI now. He is also often giving presentations at conferences and participating in podcasts or other videos with professionals in the field. Honestly, his posts run the gamut but they are each bulging with content. It's like taking a professional technical writing course that's fun, in small doses.

**Michael Andrews**

[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/michael-andrews-303720/) | [Website](https://storyneedle.com/services/)

Michael is a long-time content strategist. He's authored two books: [Metadata Basics for Web Content](https://www.amazon.com/Metadata-Basics-Web-Content-Unification-ebook/dp/B01MYH84LS/ref=books_amazonstores_desktop_mfs_aufs_ap_sc_dsk_0?_encoding=UTF8&pd_rd_w=g4dur&content-id=amzn1.sym.299f645c-0a78-440a-94a2-fb482e7cb326&pf_rd_p=299f645c-0a78-440a-94a2-fb482e7cb326&pf_rd_r=139-3778821-8747566&pd_rd_wg=gAMnF&pd_rd_r=7db022f9-1507-4465-9710-4224675bbf31), and [No More Silos: Metadata Strategy for Online Publishers](https://www.amazon.com/No-More-Silos-Metadata-Publishers-ebook/dp/B07M6QHD2Z/ref=books_amazonstores_desktop_mfs_aufs_ap_sc_dsk_1?_encoding=UTF8&pd_rd_w=EPzne&content-id=amzn1.sym.299f645c-0a78-440a-94a2-fb482e7cb326&pf_rd_p=299f645c-0a78-440a-94a2-fb482e7cb326&pf_rd_r=139-3778821-8747566&pd_rd_wg=ux6yK&pd_rd_r=f798b46e-28f7-4340-9ceb-b5e90144c935), that are both great reads. He offers a more macro analysis of content design, and has lately been writing really thought-provoking stuff on content strategy and the changes AI is bringing to it. 

**Liz Argall**

[LinkedIn](https://lizargall.github.io/docs/intro/) | [Website](https://lizargall.github.io/)

Liz is a technical writer I discovered on LinkedIn through Fabrizio. She recently started a blog, but what I really like about her writing is its focus on accessibility. As a partly disabled person myself, I find this sort of stuff invaluable. She created a [Neurodivergent Quality of Life Prioritization](https://lizargall.github.io/blog/neurodivergent) template and written on subjects like [The value of emotional intelligence in technical writing](https://lizargall.github.io/blog/emotional-intelligence). I'm looking forward to reading more of Liz's thoughts and possibly contributing to the template myself!

**Manny Silva**

[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/manuelrbsilva/) | [Website](https://www.docsastests.com/)

Manny released a book this year called [Docs As Tests: A Strategy for Relisient Documentation](https://www.amazon.com/gp/aw/d/0994169361?&linkCode=sl1&tag=hawkeyexl-20&linkId=3864c1062057ed0056f4558c193839f5&language=en_US&ref_=as_li_ss_tl), which in my opinion, breaks entirely new ground. Oh, it's true that tech writers have always had peer reviews and the revisions that go with them, or done user testing prior to publishing content, but what Manny wrote is a philosopy and practice of writing automated checks into technical documentation. Documentation is part of the product, so if we're using the same tools to manage it as code, why not test it similarly, as well? An excerpt from the introduction:

> *This book introduces a novel approach: Treating docs as tests to actively verify content against the current state of the product—rather than finding consistency issues too late through frustrated customers, faltering sales, and increased support calls.*

If you work with and/or document a lot of code daily, what Manny discusses can save you a lot of time, and headache. I've already implemented some of his advice in my recent contract, and I'm getting a lot of traction and great feedback from SMEs and stakeholders.
