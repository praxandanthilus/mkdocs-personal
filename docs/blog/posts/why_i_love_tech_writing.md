# Why I Love Being a Technical Writer

I love what I do. And I love to write.

If you didn’t know already, I’m a technical writer (my day job, I get paid to do it)! Technical writing came into my life in a familiar way. My greatest strength was writing, and my programming skills were passable. My CBEST scores (when I was laid off during the Dot-Com bust), in order from highest to lowest, were Writing > Mathematics > Reading. I knew that I didn’t want to be an engineer, and that my chances of becoming a best-selling science fiction novelist were probably slim. I worked as an intern at my dad’s software company (after he left Apple, Taligent) and it quickly became apparent that there was something my talents slotted into comfortably.

Technical writing is great because it allows me to engage in a lot of different, but vital activities daily: problem-solving, collaboration, teaching, learning, and testing.

Of course, “technical writing” covers a pretty wide range of actual disciplines, topics, and varying technical proficiencies and skills. Highly technical hardware documentation and software application user manuals often demand starkly different toolkits. Some technical writers are deep in code all day long and do much of their work on CLIs, while others work in MadCap Flare or FrameMaker, documenting mostly high-level features. But tech writers know one true thing: it’s their job to understand the product and to become an advocate for its users. 

<!-- more -->

Tech writing is all about the users. We occupy the space between the technical arcane and enlightened readers. We translate the equivalent to Sanskrit into a user’s native language with step-by-step guidance and answers to their burning questions. We are the steward of product, after those who crafted it move onto something else.

The technical landscape is always changing, but in its present incarnation there’s never been an easier time to find information. Knowledge about pretty much everything is just a Google search or a Siri query away. Documentation platforms are prolific, well documented, and often templatized. With the advent of AI, writers who don’t know any programming (or very little) can work with LLMs to write programs and mini-applications, even analyze entire codebases. 

But proliferation of information also means proliferation of misinformation. There are a lot of experts, and a lot of wannabe experts; just because a search can bring back results doesn’t mean it will be any easier for users to understand them. AI hallucinates and makes a lot of mistakes. And in the age of smart phones and on-demand information gratification, short attention spans mean communicating solutions requires more efficient and concise documentation.

In the end, every process, feature, or system to be learned ultimately requires someone to learn from. Tech writers and other information architects are the best at this. And this is the chief reason why I love the profession I chose. I work with the product every day. I have a different perspective than the engineers who built it. More importantly, I have to put on the user experience hat to learn it myself and test the docs. When I get feedback that someone found the answer they were looking for and didn’t have to call support or lasso an engineer into an hour-long meeting, I get the chills. When I get a like or a helpful comment on a page or article I wrote, the dopamine high is real. We tech writers do the hard work so that users don’t have to. You’re welcome.
