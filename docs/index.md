# About

My name is Chris Castro. I’ve been writing professionally in one form or another since my college days. I grew up in the Bay Area, surrounded by tech, and so it suffused nearly every aspect of my life. My dad worked for Apple—I cut my chops on HyperCard, BASIC, and QuickTime. I learned the basics of object-oriented programming from BBSes, issues of MacWorld, and trial-and-error. Sure, I played outside just like other kids, but my true love was the computer. 

The breadth of my experience in technical writing falls in the SaaS category: at Selectica and Conga I authored user documentation for SaaS platforms and launched the API documentation initiatives for [Conga’s Developer Hub](https://developer.conga.com/?_ga=2.267933582.1275441739.1765744898-1742091688.1765744898). This is where I learned how to really work with SOAP, REST, and the OpenAPI specification. I’m a Confluence power user, and well-versed in DITA and CMSes.

In the mid-2010s I took a job with Rivian’s manufacturing engineering organization, a large departure for me. I fell in love with robotics and autonomy software. I instituted a tightly-controlled and versioned standards documentation ecosystem, crafted proposals for equipment naming conventions and MES systems, deployed PLC add-on instructions using Sphinx/RST, and authored a complex RFP/RFQ template for Global Supply Managers with contributions from multiple cross-org stakeholders. 

At Blue River Technology I’ve worked on an SDK for their automation platform, documenting embedded software and computer vision/machine learning logic. I migrated their existing documentation from Confluence/Google Docs to an S3-hosted website generated with Material for MkDocs. I’m currently doing the same for their autonomous tractor program and contributing to a Python module that builds interactive requirements traceability into their systems documentation. 

I use Microsoft Copilot/Windsurf and Google Gemini daily. I believe that AI has already become intricately entwined with every aspect of the tech industry, and as a technical writer it’s my job to learn how to work with it just as I would any other tool. AI might know a lot, but they have to learn from experts. Tech writers write for users—AI trains on our content, too. 

In my free time, I am a member of the board of the [Society for the Promotion of Speculative Fiction](http://www.societysf.org/), and an organizer for the local, fan-run scifi convention [BayCon](http://www.baycon.org/). I also sell collectibles on [TCGPlayer](https://www.tcgplayer.com/search/all/product?seller=cfc33998&view=grid) and [eBay](https://www.ebay.com/usr/ccastro1). I love board gaming, science fiction, and Twin Peaks.

I tell stories on my blog. I would love to connect with employers who are doing great things, especially progressive organizations and clean energy. And other writers, too! We can learn so much from each other. In fact, we should.
